Beyond Good and Evil

Nietzsche, Friedrich. Beyond Good and Evil. Translated by Marion Faber. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1998.

Philosophy is a tyrannical drive itself, the most spiritual form of the will to power, to 'creation of the world,' to the first cause.

Nietzsche 1998, 11

Every act of willing is simply a matter of commanding and obeying, based on a social structure of many souls: for this reason a philosopher should claim the right to comprehend willing from within the sphere of ethics: ethics, that is, understood as the theory of hierachical relationships among which the phenomenon 'life' has its origins.

Nietzsche 1998, 20

Philosophy is thus a kind of atavism of the highest order. This explains the strange family resemblence of all Indian, Greek, and German philosophizing.

Nietzsche 1998, 20

The 'unfree' will is mythology: in real life it is only a matter of strong and weak wills.

Nietzsche 1998, 21

Every exceptional person instinctively seeks out his fortress, his secrecy, where he is delivered from the crowd... where he is allowed to forget the rule of 'humanity'; being an exception to it; in one case, however, an even stranger instinct pushes him, as a person of great and exceptional knowledge, to this rule.

Nietzsche 1998, 27

Only a very few people can be independent; it is a prerogative of the strong.

Nietzsche 1998, 30

Books for the masses are always bad smelling books; the odour of little people clings to them.

Nietzsche 1998, 31

Those of us who are destined to be independent and to command must in return set ourselves our own tests... We must know how to preserve ourselves: the greatest test of independance.

Nietzsche 1998, 39

In the end, if you wnat to know something, you must do everything yourself, which means there is much to do.

Nietzsche 1998, 43

Only through the study of history is a scholar able to treat religions with a reverant seriousness and a certain shy regard.

Nietzsche 1998, 52

Piety... can turn humans so completely into art, surface, opulesence, kindness, that we no longer suffer when we look at them.

Nietzsche 1998, 53

A true teacher doesn't take anything serious except in relation to his pupils - not even himself.

Nietzsche 1998, 58

Our vanity is more resistant to wounds when our pride has just been wounded.

Nietzsche 1998, 69
