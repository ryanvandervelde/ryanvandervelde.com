Eudemian Ethics

Aristotle. Eudemian Ethics. Translated by Sir Anthony Kenny. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2011.

Happiness is the noblest, the best, and the most pleasant of human goods.

Aristotle 2011, 3

A statesman is someone who chooses noble deeds for their own sake, wheras most politicians take up their career for personal gain and advancement.

Aristotle 2011, 7

There are two ways in which things fall within the scope of action. There are things that are the purposes of our action, and the things that we do in order to bring them about.

Aristotle 2011, 10

Let us postulate that the work of the soul is to be alive or to exercise life while awake, since in sleep the soul is idling and at rest.

Aristotle 2011, 15

It is the things that are in our power that are objects of choice and conduct.

Aristotle 2011, 32

It is the courageous man who dares only when reason tells him too.

Aristotle 2011, 39

Barbarian courage in general involves an element of rage.

Aristotle 2011, 40

If things are to be exchanged, they must somehow be capable of comparison. That is why money was invented, and it has become a kind of mean.

Aristotle 2011, 61

A man acts justly or unjustly if he does the appropriate acts voluntarily... By voluntarily I mean something within his power that a man does knowingly.

Aristotle 2011, 66

Overall the answer to the question 'can man treat himself unjustly?' is given by the distinctions we made in the response to 'can one voluntarily be treated unjustly?'

Aristotle 2011, 73

A person knows something when he has a certain kind of belief and when he is aware of the principles that support it... Let this, be our definition of knowledge.

Aristotle 2011, 77

One might also query why a boy may become a mathemitician, but not a philosopher or physicist. It is because the objects of mathematics are abstract, while the principles of the other disciplines are denied from experience.

Aristotle 2011, 82

It is not possible to be wise without being good.

Aristotle 2011, 87

Some people are addicted to entertainment: these are generally regarded as incontinent, but in fact they are soft, because entertainment is a relaxation, an interuption to one's work.

Aristotle 2011, 102

Knowledge alone does not make a person wise; he has to act on it.

Aristotle 2011, 105

The pleasures of thinking and learning make us think and learn all the more.

Aristotle 2011, 108

Mere wanting to be healthy does not make people healthy, and neither does wanting to be friends make people friends.

Aristotle 2011, 119

Pleasure glues bad men to eachother.

Aristotle 2011, 121

For every individual self-perception and self knowledge is the most desirable of all things, and that is why an appetite for life is inborn in each of us, for living must be regarded as a kind of knowledge.

Aristotle 2011, 137

One always desires to live because one always desires to know

Aristotle 2011, 138

It is difficult not only to get many friends-since they have to be tried and tested-but also to enjoy them when you have them.

Aristotle 2011, 139
