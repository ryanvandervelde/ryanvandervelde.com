On the Soul

Aristotle. De Anima (On the Soul). Translated by Hugh Lawson-Tancred. London: Penguin Books, 1986.

A craft must us its tools, and a soul must use its body.

Aristotle 1986, 12

Old age does not occur because the sould has been affected in some way, but because the body in which it resides has been affected.

Aristotle 1986, 14

For both sleep and waking depend on the presence of the soul, and waking is analogous to contemplating. While sleep is analogous to possessing knowledge but not actually using it.

Aristotle 1986, 21

Why is it under one's own control to think whenever one wishes, but it is not under one's control to perceive.

Aristotle 1986, 32

Since sight is the premier sense, imagination has received its name from light because without light it is impossible to see. And because imaginings persist according to them, some (namely beasts) because they are not capable of thought, and others (namely human beings) because their mind is clouded at times by passion, sickness, or sleep.

Aristotle 1986, 55

Perceiving then, is like more asserting and thinking; and whenever an object is pleasant or painful, the soul pursues or avoids it, as if it were affirming or denying.

Aristotle 1986, 59

And for this reason one could not learn or comprehend anything without perceiving something; and whenever one contemplates, one must at the same time contemplate a sort of image; for images are like percepts, except that they are without matter.

Aristotle 1986, 61

For theoretical thought does not contemplate what is to be done, nor does it say anything about what is to be avoided or pursued, but movement always belongs to a thing which avoids or pursues something. But neither is it the case that, whenever thought contemplates this sort of object, it straight away commands that one avoid or pursue it... Furthur, even when thought does give an order and cognition does tell one to avoid or pursue something, one is not moved but instead act according to appetite, as is the case of people with no self-control.

Aristotle 1986, 63

Desire, too, is in every case for the sake of something; for the object of desire is the starting point of practical thought, and its last step is the starting point of action.

Aristotle 1986, 64

Everything is moved by pushing and pulling.

Aristotle 1986, 65

For people with good memories are not the same as those who are good at recollecting, but, for the most part, those are quick and learn easily are better at recollecting.

Aristotle 1986, 95

And for this reason sometimes we do not know, what such movements take place in our soul as a result of past perception, whether they correspond to what we have perceived, and we are in doubt whether it is memory or not. But sometimes it happens that we think and recollect that we have heard or seen something before. And this happens whenever one turns from contemplating an objects in itself to comtemplating it as related to something else.

Aristotle 1986, 98

Recollecting differs from remembering not only in regard to time but also because, while many other animals partake of memory, no animal of which we are aware, it is fair to say, except for a human being, partakes of recollection. The reason is that recollecting is a sort of inference; for when a person expierenced something of the sort before, and this is like a sort of inquiry.

Aristotle 1986, 102

People who possess large upper bodies, including dwarfish persons, have poorer memories than those with contrary stature because they carry a heavy weight upon their perceptive faculty.

Aristotle 1986, 102
